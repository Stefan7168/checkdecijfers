# Product vision

## The problem

Official Dutch statistics are free, authoritative — and effectively locked away. CBS StatLine is *"berucht ingewikkeld"*: finding one number means knowing which of thousands of cryptically named tables (`85552NED`, `RegioS='GM0344'`) holds it, decoding dimension codes, and hoping the table wasn't redesigned mid-year. A journalist on deadline spends 45 minutes clicking, or — worse — asks a general-purpose AI chatbot that confidently invents a number. In journalism a wrong number is a *doodszonde*: a public correction, a damaged reputation, possibly a legal claim.

## The product

**checkdecijfers.nl** (working name, see Decision log) is a chat interface over official CBS statistics where **the AI never calculates or interprets the numbers**. The AI parses the question and explains the result; deterministic code computes the answer from CBS data ingested into our own database. Every number is traceable to an official CBS cell, and every answer shows its source table and freshness date.

A user gets, per question:

1. **A validated answer** — the exact number(s), computed by code, not by a language model.
2. **Source attribution** — CBS table ID and title, the date we last synchronized that table, and the period the data covers.
3. **A simple chart** when the question is about a trend or comparison.
4. **A refusal or a clarifying question** when the data is missing, ambiguous, or stale. The product never guesses.

The core promise, in one sentence: **every number traceable to an official CBS cell, with source and date shown.** This is the claim we make publicly (interview Q5). The notes' slogans ("0% hallucinatiegarantie", "De AI die niet liegt") overpromise in absolute terms and were set aside as the public claim; whether they ever appear in marketing is Stefan's call after legal/comms review ([open-questions.md](open-questions.md) #8).

Out of scope for v1 but deliberately kept alive (see [06-roadmap.md](06-roadmap.md) and the "Future-build seams" section of [04-architecture.md](04-architecture.md)): social-format exports, interactive chart studio, shareable answer pages with OpenGraph images (programmatic SEO), embeds, alerts, enterprise house-style charts.

## Who it's for

**v1 audience: Dutch journalists — freelancers and small newsrooms first** (interview Q2). The Netherlands has roughly 15,000–17,000 active journalists; the underserved segment is freelancers and small/regional newsrooms for whom LocalFocus-style corporate contracts are out of reach. Their documented buying behavior anchors the business model: **€5–7.50 is the maximum for an everyday article; anything under €10 fits newsroom "kleine onkosten" expense rules without approval.** Their alternative — 45 minutes of self-searching StatLine — costs a newsroom €30–45 in time.

Large newsrooms (NOS, DPG Media, Mediahuis) are a later enterprise tier, not v1.

**The pivot we considered and parked.** The notes contain a serious critique of this audience (small TAM — "a few hundred data journalists"; churn risk; Microsoft Copilot could ship a native CBS integration) and a researched pivot to real-estate/policy B2B (€250–1,000/mo willingness to pay, but requiring Kadaster/PDOK/KvK enrichment pipelines — a far bigger data build). Decision (interview Q2): journalists first; the vertical pivot stays in the roadmap as an explicit Phase 3+ option with trigger conditions, because "we can always expand / rebrand later." The risks from the critique are recorded below as standing risks, not reasons to stall.

## Positioning

| Alternative | What it is | Why we win |
|---|---|---|
| **CBS StatLine** | The authoritative source, free | 100% reliable but a maze; we sell speed and interpretation on top of the same data, with attribution back to it |
| **Generic AI chat** (ChatGPT, Perplexity, Copilot) | Fast, conversational | Hallucination risk; cites news articles instead of source tables; cannot guarantee cell-level traceability — our entire architecture exists to be the opposite |
| **LocalFocus** (ANP) | Data-visualization platform for newsrooms | Expensive corporate contracts, visualization-first, no Q&A; we serve the freelancers and small newsrooms it ignores |
| **Nederland in Beeld** | Automated open-data platform (CBS/IND/COA pipelines) | Architecture role model (own database, "no number generated by AI", per-chart source links) — but a **thematic, activist platform with a declared standpoint** on migration costs. We are the neutral, general-purpose, question-first counterpart |
| **AlleCijfers.nl, Waarstaatjegemeente.nl** | Pre-built dashboards | Click-and-filter UX for pre-chosen questions; we answer *your* question |

Positioning summary — the notes' **"Drie B's van de Journalistiek"**: *Betrouwbaarheid* (deterministic numbers, no hallucination by construction), *Bronvermelding* (exact CBS table code with every answer), *Betaalbaarheid* (credits, not enterprise licenses).

## Business model (summary)

Decided in the interview (Q3); full phasing in [06-roadmap.md](06-roadmap.md). **Nothing here is built in Phase 0.**

- **Credit packs, no individual subscription.** Journalists' demand is irregular; a monthly fee feels like *"weggegooid geld"* in quiet months. Landing-page promise: no subscription, no fine print.
- **Credits never expire** (decided; the notes' 1-year-expiry idea solved an accounting-liability problem we won't have at this scale).
- **Indicative prices, easy to change** (decided: working numbers, not commitments): ~€0.50/credit; packs around €15 and €39; 1 credit = simple check, 3 = analysis + visual, 5 = heavy analysis; a complete article check ≈ €3–5. A small free allowance funnels new users.
- **Cost transparency before spend**: the user sees the credit cost of an action *before* the expensive step runs — estimate label, confirmation for heavy queries, visible tariff card. A clarifying counter-question never costs a credit.
- **Later tiers** (Phase 3+): newsroom license (flat fee, email-domain access, ~€250–500/mo indicative), premium audit-trail exports, alerts. **Rejected**: Bring-Your-Own-API-key (kills the conversion funnel; the notes reached this conclusion themselves).

## Standing risks (recorded, monitored — not blockers)

1. **Big-tech moat risk**: Microsoft Copilot / OpenAI shipping reliable native CBS integration would erode the standalone product. Mitigation: the moat is the maintained translation layer (CBS codes → concepts) and the validation pipeline, not the chat UI. Revisit trigger: a major assistant demonstrably answers CBS questions with correct cell-level attribution.
2. **TAM risk**: the paying journalist market may be as small as the burn critique claims. Revisit trigger: Phase 2 conversion data; the vastgoed/policy pivot is the documented response.
3. **CBS platform risk**: table redesigns, mid-year schema changes, announced SDMX migration — handled in [05-data-rules.md](05-data-rules.md) and ADR [003](decisions/003-cbs-access-layer.md).
4. **CBS modernizes StatLine's UX** — would shrink the pain we monetize. Low probability short-term; monitor.
5. **Trust liability**: one publicized wrong answer damages the core promise disproportionately. Mitigation: the principles, the benchmark gate in [03-mvp-scope.md](03-mvp-scope.md), and refusal-over-guessing.
